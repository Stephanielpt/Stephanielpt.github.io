FACEBOOK  UNIVERSITY  SUMMER  2018

  • Technology

    August 26, 2018

  • Facebook University introduced me to the most inspiring and passionate people this summer. Within my cohort, I found amazing friends, and the best teammates I could have asked for. Within the first three weeks, we ramped up from 0 to 100, and built three apps, just to learn some iOS basics. During the rest of the summer, I worked with my two teammates to develop a full-fledged app from start to finish. We chose everything from the big idea to every little feature, which was one of the coolest parts for me.

    In some ways, the summer felt like a 2 month long hackathon, and my team's final product was "Fetch." Fetch is a map-based, item-sharing app. It's practical, and straighforward - it targets the pain-points of renting, but most importantly, it's something we had worked towards and devoted significant time to for 5 weeks. During that time, I was learning more than just technical iOS skills, I was also learning about how to organize a long term project. We created sprints, user stories, and git issues and branches for our project, and they helped us work well as a team and stay focused on our goals. After 5 weeks of development, we demoed to Denise Hernandez, and were ecstatic to find out that we were chosen to demo Fetch again that week, but this time, to Mark Zuckerberg.

    On my last day, it was so hard to say goodbye, but I knew I was walking away with new friends, new inspiration, and a new outlook that would stick with me long after the end of my internship.

Time Saver: Python  Web  Scraper

  • Technology

    October 15, 2017

  • Ding! I look over at my phone. "IT'S UP IT'S UP!!! One spot in Ballroom class just opened!"

    Although this may sound like it came from an overexcited friend, it actually came from my web scraper through the Twilio API.

    I use my webscraper for everything from snagging spots in popular classes to being able to watch Riverdale within 10 seconds of it becoming available online. I don't have check my favorite websites incessantly anymore, because my phone texts me when changes are made to them. It saves me little pockets of time in my day, which can add up in the long run!

    It works just like ISR's we learned about in electrical engineering. At the beginning of the year, we had our software constantly checking on the status of all the hardware parts from a while loop. This is called polling, and as I soon realized - extremely inefficient. Later in the year, we programmed ISR's or interrupt service routines. Interrupts let the program know when a key change has occurred by interrupting the program, and this makes keeping tabs on hardware much easier - and time efficient.

    The project was also connected to my ventures in web development. The same identifiers that I used to format my HTML could be used to target specific information on a long cluttered website. This parsing turned out to be pretty helpful for me and my friends this semester when registering for classes, and I'm sure it will continue to keep up to date with whatever life throws my way.

Computer Model of a Six-star Orbiting System

  • Technology

    July 16, 2016

  • This summer I created a computer model of a six-star orbiting system.

    Not going to lie, it was incredibly intimidating at first, but it proved to be just as fun as it was challenging. I had to use the Newton's Laws of Gravitation to dictate how stars reacted to the others stars nearby. On top of that, I had to use 3D trigonometry to ensure the stars all started in an orientation that would allow them to maintain orbit around each other. We wouldn't want all of them to be attracted to an abnormally massive star and collide!

    The orbits turned out to be beautiful and I was even able to create a self-sustaining nine-star orbiting system (see above!)

In the Kitchen

  • Hobbies

    May 27, 2017

  • I've always been conscious about what foods I put into my body. As a swimmer I had to be, but this summer I decided to conquer the kitchen, to prepare my own meals and fuel my body with only the best ingredients. I decided to start my cooking adventures in Greece, and my first dish was a baked Meditteranean Chicken. The recipe called for at least 15 different spices and my mom taught me that in cooking I could be a little more creative than in baking. We altered the recipe and added some lemon juice and white wine to enhance the flavors and voila! Success! We ate it with everything from pita to french bread and found it went especially well with avocado.

    I followed it up with several other Meditterarean style dishes that often starred tomatoes, cucumbers, garbanzo beans, feta, and of course, lemon juice and oregano.

    My next culinary destination was influenced by Cubano, Erick Brian Colón. I made typical cuban Frijoles Negroes and put them in flavorful Arroz Morros. And of course, Pastelitos de Guyaba were eaten and Cuban Tostadas were made.

    There's something so relaxing about preparing a meal, and there's something so satisfying about eating it afterwards.
