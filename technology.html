iOS Development:  Creating   the  World's  Largest   Whiteboard   at  Treehacks

  • Technology

    February 20, 2018

  • In my junior year of high school, I became coach of the math team. It was when I discovered my passion for teaching. In the last couple years, combining technology and education was one of the dreams that pushed me towards coding.

    Since then, I continued to coach math, tutored in physics and French, and started a computer science club to introduce kids to programming earlier. I couldn’t get enough.

    Even as a teacher though, I was still a student - I learned about what makes a good teacher, and how to convey difficult concepts in a simple manner. My biggest realization was what I call the whiteboard effect. I began to notice that my kids had better ideas, more confidence, and more fun when I called them up to the whiteboard to solve problems. And I called them all up at the same time, not one by one. This way, everyone could see each other working. My kids were seeing other kids get stuck in the same places they were, and unafraid to ask questions. They worked together and complemented each other, because one person might forget the distance formula in the beginning, but be the one who remembers the shoelace theorem needed at the end.

    Most importantly, they felt more confident in themselves when they could work together to find the answer without asking for my help. I loved seeing the spark in their eyes when they were able to help their peers and solve the most difficult question I threw their way.

    The whiteboard effect worked perfectly for my class of 6 student. But it wasn’t as effective with 20 students - and practically useless for 200 students in a college lecture hall. I’ve wanted to use technology to create “the world’s largest whiteboard” for a while now, and at Treehacks I finally started the journey.

    I chose Swift for iOS development as my vehicle for change. Most students bring their iPhones to class anyway, so why not use them to learn! Although I had never dealt with mobile development before, I managed to get the app off the ground within the 36 hours, I created an app that allowed a user to enroll into a classroom and then draw on their own board and swipe into other student’s boards to see how they were going about finding a solution.

    There’s still a lot to do in backend with the app, but I feel great to have finally started!! I know that this will be a continuing project and can’t wait to delve further into the world of iOS development to solve an issue that has directly impacted my life.

    2/21/18 Update! Drawing with colors are now a thing :)

Time Saver: Python  Web  Scraper

  • Technology

    October 15, 2017

  • Ding! I look over at my phone. "IT'S UP IT'S UP!!! One spot in Ballroom class just opened!"

    Although this may sound like it came from an overexcited friend, it actually came from my web scraper through the Twilio API.

    I use my webscraper for everything from snagging spots in popular classes to being able to watch Riverdale within 10 seconds of it becoming available online. I don't have check my favorite websites incessantly anymore, because my phone texts me when changes are made to them. It saves me little pockets of time in my day, which can add up in the long run!

    It works just like ISR's we learned about in electrical engineering. At the beginning of the year, we had our software constantly checking on the status of all the hardware parts from a while loop. This is called polling, and as I soon realized - extremely inefficient. Later in the year, we programmed ISR's or interrupt service routines, that made keeping tabs on hardware (or episodes of Riverdale) much easier - and time efficient.

    The project also connected to my ventures in web development. The same identifiers that I used to format my HTML can be used to target specific information on a long cluttered website. This parsing turned out to be pretty helpful for me and my friends this semester when registering for classes, and I'm sure it'll continue to keep me up to date with whatever life throws my way.

Computer  Model  of  a  Six-star  Orbiting System

  • Technology

    July 16, 2016

  • This summer I created a computer model of a six-star orbiting system.

    Not going to lie, it was incredibly intimidating at first, but it proved to be just as fun as it was challenging. I had to use the Newton's Laws of Gravitation to dictate how stars reacted to the others stars nearby. On top of that, I had to use 3D trigonometry to ensure the stars all started in an orientation that would allow them to maintain orbit around each other. We wouldn't want all of them to be attracted to an abnormally massive star and collide!
